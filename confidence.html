Finding The Confidence To Write

On Writing

Writing is hard. Like, it's genuinely difficult. I’ve been staring at this page for a good ten minutes, struggling to figure out where I should start, what this first lesson should even be about.

So I’m going with this.

Writing is hard.

And I think that gets forgotten sometimes? Because for many of us, we're constantly writing. Typing. Yelling at friends in the group chat, yelling at strangers on Twitter-- that’s writing. You’re taking your thoughts and communicating them, and you’re doing it in a way that feels natural and intuitive and effortless.

But sitting down to write a story, or an essay, or even an email? Suddenly, something’s different. Suddenly, it’s not so effortless. And if you don't recognise that, and understand why it's happening, it's honestly hard to escape.

See, most writers — most artists, really — are good at critically analysing things. They've got a good eye for what works and what doesn't, especially in their chosen medium. And that applies to people who are just starting out.

Ira Glass covered this a while back--

All of us who do creative work, we get into it because we have good taste. But there is this gap. For the first couple years you make stuff, it’s just not that good. It’s trying to be good, it has potential, but it’s not. But your taste, the thing that got you into the game, is still killer. And your taste is why your work disappoints you.

If you can't relate to any of this, then genuinely, I'm glad. You can probably tap out of the lesson here. But for myself, and for a lot of writers, creating something that you know isn't as good as it could be? It feels excruciating. Because you are clever! You know what's good, and you know what you just typed isn't. You think you should be able to create something great. But you can't. And it's grueling to run up against that, again and again.

I say all this for two reasons.

Firstly, if you find writing difficult and anxious, I want to make sure you know you aren't alone. Most writers I've talked to have dealt with this to some degree. Some big name writers still do.

And secondly? Because I know firsthand that the only way past this sort of anxiety is by recognising it and confronting it. And that doesn't mean forcing yourself to write. It doesn't mean punishing yourself if you can't write. It means introspecting, and learning what works for you.

So this is lesson zero. I'm deciding that now. We're not going to cover any theory this time. I'm just going to talk about the things that have helped me unwind some of that anxiety.

Are you all down with that? Great. Fantastic.

Half Measures

Wait, okay, quick diversion. There are a lot of things that I've done that are adjacent to writing, and did relieve some anxiety, but never actually helped me write.

Things like...

  • Telling people about the thing I'm writing.
  • Making mood boards on Pinterest.
  • Making playlists on Spotify.
  • Organising files and folders on my computer.
  • Rewording old lines, because they don't sound quite right.
  • Playing with margins and fonts.

And so forth.

Reducing your anxiety is important. But these sort of middle ground things have only ever exhausted me creatively? And they never seem to leave me feeling better afterwards.

If you want to do those things, great. But don't let them interfere with your writing, and definitely don't let them interfere with the time you should be setting aside to relax and recover.

Full Measures

Alright. Here's what I've found helps me write.

Schedules

Setting schedules can help. Telling yourself you'll write five hundred words every day, that sort of thing.

In my experience, though, it's a little bit easier to commit to a length of time, rather than a number of words? So...

Setting A Timer

That burst of focus you have on the night an essay's due? Yeah. That. You're going to harness that.

Set yourself a timer for however long... and just write. Close everything else, turn off your WiFi, whatever. It doesn't matter how much you get done. You just need to learn how to sit down and start busting out words.

I'd highly recommend checking out Do The Write Thing, a writing podcast. They give you a prompt every week, and challenge you to write a story in thirty minutes. Plus, if you post what you write on their subreddit, the hosts will often give you feedback on the podcast! Which is really neat!

Cutting Corners

Deadlines and pressure force you to make compromises — you let a line be bad because you don't have time to fix it. But intentionally doing that has helped me, too.

Just giving up on capital letters and punctuation, letting my characters sound like awkward buffoons, because I know that I can come back in ten minutes once I have a rough draft and fix things up.

It can be tough at times. It's a learned skill. But I find it's usually faster for me to write an awful draft and then completely rewrite it into a good one, than it is for me to write something perfect from scratch.

Decide What You Want To Improve On

This is kind of important in general-- if you want to improve as a writer, it really helps to decide what you want to improve on? Like, dialogue, action scenes, whatever. And then just throw yourself into a scene that features that heavily.

I've found this helps me in two ways. It makes it easier to start writing, because I already know at least partially what I'm writing about.

But it's also just easier to improve. Because it doesn't matter as much if everything else is terrible, as long as the dialogue's okay. Or, hell, the dialogue can suck. But as long as it's a little better than it was this time last month? Then that's improvement, and it's something to be proud of yourself for.

Being Kind To Yourself

Don't tie your value or identity into writing. If you write something that doesn't measure up to your expectations, forgive yourself. If you can't find a way to write, then forgive yourself for that, too.

Keep Working

Truth is, the only way you're going to get better at writing... is by writing. Reading theory can help. Talking about writing can help. But you need to put those things into practice, otherwise you'll never make progress.

That Ira Glass quote before? It wasn't complete. I cut off the end. For emphasis.

So here's the rest of it.

A lot of people never get past this phase, they quit. Most people I know who do interesting, creative work went through years of this. We know our work doesn’t have this special thing that we want it to have. We all go through this.

And if you are just starting out or you are still in this phase, you gotta know its normal and the most important thing you can do is do a lot of work. Put yourself on a deadline so that every week you will finish one story. It is only by going through a volume of work that you will close that gap, and your work will be as good as your ambitions. And I took longer to figure out how to do this than anyone I’ve ever met. It’s gonna take awhile. It’s normal to take awhile. You’ve just gotta fight your way through.

You're never going to improve until you put that work in. But if you find you can't put that work in? There's probably something blocking you.
